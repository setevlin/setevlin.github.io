In this assignment, you'll analyze a collection of data sets from the San Francisco Open Data Portal and Zillow. The data sets have been stored in the SQLite database sf_data.sqlite, which you can download here. The database contains the following tables:

Table Description
crime Crime reports dating back to 2010.
mobile_food_locations List of all locations where mobile food vendors sell.
mobile_food_permits List of all mobile food vendor permits. More details here.
mobile_food_schedule Schedules for mobile food vendors.
noise Noise complaints dating back to August 2015.
parking List of all parking lots.
parks List of all parks.
schools List of all schools.
zillow Zillow rent and housing statistics dating back to 1996. More details here.

The mobile_food_ tables are explicitly connected through the locationid and permit columns. The other tables are not connected, but you may be able to connect them using dates, latitude/longitude, or postal codes.

Shapefiles for US postal codes are available here. These may be useful for converting latitude/longitude to postal codes.

Shapefiles for San Francisco Neighborhoods are available here.

It is obvious that houses within the 94104 zipcode have the highest median price per square foot at around 1,336 dollars per square foot, which make homes in this zipcode the most expensive homes in San Francisco. The 94104 zipcode is a small area between the Financial District and Chinatown. It makes sense that this location would be such an expensive place to live because of the easy access to popular San Francisco locations. The second most expensive location to buy a home is in the 94105 zipcode, where the average median price per square foot is 798 dollars. This location is right below the Financial District but also hugs the coastline by the Oakland Bay Bridge, so between the easy access to popular San Francisco locations, some of the houses in this zipcode have a view of the San Francisco Bay, which is also worth a lot in terms of property value. The third most expensive location to live in San Francisco is within the area code 94123, which comes in at 696 dollars per square foot. This zipcode is in the Marina District, which again has access to popular locations, such as the Fisherman's Wharf, and it also has views of the water from specific locations.

The least expensive place to live in San Francisco are neighborhoods within the 94124 zipcode, at around 297 dollars per square foot. This zipcode includes locations like Hunters Point, which is a dangerous neighborhood. The next most inexpensive places to live is 94134, 347 dollars per square foot, which is also surrounded by areas that are not very safe, such as Hunters Point and the Outer Mission District. Lastly, the third least expensive zipcode to live within is 94080, around 376 dollars per square foot, which is South San Francisco. It may be cheaper to live there because you are outside of San Francisco proper.

Overall, locations that are close to popular locations around San Francisco, such as the wharf or the financial district, seem to have higher median prices per square foot on average. And locations that are father away from the popular locations and are surrounded by more dangerous neighborhoods, tend to have lower median prices per square foot.

This table shows that the most number of vendors sell their product on Mondays. There are still a lot of choices for vendors throughout the other weekdays. But on the weekends, there are significantly less food trucks available to visit. This was surpising to me because I would have thought the opposite pattern would occur. The lack of vendors on the weekends may be due to an increase in permit price to sell their products on weekends or from lack of parking throughout the city for their trucks. However, it does make sense for vendors to be available throughout the week because that means that vendors are available to sell to people during their lunch break or on their communte if they are walking to and from work or school.

I did find it surprising that there were as many push carts throughout the city as there are. I figured that the number of food trucks would disproportionately outweigh the number of push carts. However, it does make sense because push carts are significantly less expensive to run than a food truck and are easier to manuever within the streets of San Francisco.
